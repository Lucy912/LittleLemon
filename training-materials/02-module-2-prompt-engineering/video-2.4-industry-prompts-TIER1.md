### [1:15-2:00] CATEGORY 1: MARKETING & CONTENT (8 prompts)

**These cover the most common marketing tasks:**

**1. Content Strategy**
"Act as a content strategist with B2B expertise.

Task: Create a 90-day content plan for [platform: LinkedIn/blog/YouTube].

Context: Target audience is [who]. Main pain points: [pain 1, pain 2, pain 3]. Our differentiation: [what makes us unique]. Goal: [leads/awareness/authority].

Format: Table with columns: Week, Topic, Content Type, Keyword, CTA, Success Metric. Then explain the strategic flow.

Tone: Strategic and practical, focused on conversion not vanity metrics."

**2. Social Media Post**
"Act as a social media manager known for high engagement B2B content.

Task: Write a [LinkedIn/Twitter] post about [topic/announcement].

Context: [Brief context about what happened and why it matters]. Audience: [who]. Goal: [engagement/clicks/applications].

Format: Hook (1 line) + Story/insight (2-3 short paragraphs) + Key takeaway + Question to prompt comments. Max [characters].

Tone: Conversational but professional, human not corporate, vulnerable where appropriate."

**3. Email Marketing Campaign**
"Act as an email marketing specialist with expertise in [industry].

Task: Write a 5-email nurture sequence for [leads who downloaded X/trial users/churned customers].

Context: Goal is to [convert to paid/reactivate/book demo]. Main objection: [what stops them]. Our key differentiator: [why choose us].

Format: For each email provide: Subject line, Preview text, Body (3 paragraphs max), CTA. Then explain the sequence strategy.

Tone: Helpful not salesy, addressing objections without being defensive, building trust through value."

**4. Blog Post Outline**
"Act as a content writer who creates comprehensive, SEO-optimized blog posts.

Task: Create a detailed outline for a blog post on [topic].

Context: Target keyword: [keyword]. Search intent: [informational/commercial]. Audience: [who] searching for [what]. Competing posts: [common angles]. Our unique angle: [differentiation].

Format: Title + Meta description + 8-10 H2 headings with 2-3 bullet points per section + Internal linking opportunities + CTA.

Tone: Authoritative but accessible, practical not academic, focused on reader value."

**5. Ad Copy (Google/Facebook)**
"Act as a direct response copywriter specializing in [Google Ads/Facebook Ads].

Task: Write [number] ad variations for [product/service].

Context: Target audience: [who]. Pain point: [problem]. Offer: [solution/discount/trial]. Character limits: [headline: X, description: Y]. Competitors saying: [common messages]. Our angle: [differentiation].

Format: For each ad: Headline + Description + Display URL + Why this angle works.

Tone: Clear and benefit-focused, addressing pain immediately, creating urgency without being pushy."

### [2:00-2:45] CATEGORY 2: SALES & CUSTOMER SUCCESS (8 prompts)

**1. Sales Outreach Email (Cold)**
"Act as a B2B sales professional known for high cold email response rates.

Task: Write a cold outreach email to [job title] at [company type].

Context: We help [target] solve [problem]. Their likely pain: [specific pain based on industry/role]. Personalization hook: [something you noticed about them/their company]. Ask: [meeting/demo/resource].

Format: Subject line + 3 short paragraphs (2-3 sentences each) + Clear ask + P.S. with social proof.

Tone: Respectful of their time, personalizing, focused on their problem not our product, confident not desperate."

**2. Demo Prep & Discovery Questions**
"Act as a sales engineer who runs consultative product demos.

Task: Create a demo preparation guide for [prospect company] in [industry].

Context: Company size: [size]. Current solution: [competitor/manual process]. Their stated pain: [pain]. Decision maker: [role]. Budget: [range]. Timeline: [urgency].

Format:
1. Pre-demo research summary
2. 5 discovery questions to ask first
3. Demo flow (features to show in order)
4. Objections likely to arise + responses
5. Closing questions

Tone: Consultative not salesy, focused on their outcome not our features."

**3. Follow-up After No Response**
"Act as a persistent but respectful sales professional.

Task: Write a follow-up email after no response to [proposal/demo/initial outreach].

Context: Last contact was [timeframe] ago. We discussed [brief summary]. They expressed interest in [goal]. Common reasons for no response in our industry: [busy/changed priorities/budget]. Relationship level: [warm/cold].

Format: Subject line + Brief reminder + Value add (new insight/resource/data) + Low-pressure re-ask.

Tone: Understanding and helpful, acknowledging they're busy, providing new value not just 'checking in'."

**4. Handling Objection: Price**
"Act as a sales coach who helps reps overcome the price objection.

Task: Create a response framework for when prospects say 'This is too expensive.'

Context: Our price: [amount]. Market average: [amount]. Typical ROI: [X months to payback]. What's included: [value items]. What cheaper alternatives miss: [gaps].

Format:
1. Acknowledge their concern
2. Reframe from cost to investment
3. Break down the ROI
4. Compare to cost of not solving the problem
5. Offer options (annual vs monthly, different tiers)
6. Trial close question

Tone: Confident in value, empathetic to budget constraints, consultative not defensive."

**5. Customer Success Check-in**
"Act as a customer success manager focused on proactive retention.

Task: Write a check-in email for customers who are [30 days in/90 days in/showing low usage].

Context: Customer is [company name], [industry], [size]. They're using [features], not using [features]. Success looks like [outcome]. Risk signals: [low login/no key feature adoption/support tickets].

Format: Personalized subject + Show you're paying attention (specific usage data) + Offer help + Specific next step (call/resource/training).

Tone: Genuinely helpful, proactive not reactive, partner not vendor."

### [2:45-3:30] CATEGORY 3: PRODUCT & ENGINEERING (8 prompts)

**1. Product Requirements Document (PRD)**
"Act as a senior product manager known for clear, comprehensive PRDs.

Task: Create a PRD for [feature/product].

Context: User problem: [pain]. Current workaround: [what users do now]. Business goal: [metric impact]. Technical constraints: [limitations]. Stakeholders: [who needs to approve].

Format:
1. Problem statement (2-3 sentences)
2. User stories (3-5 with acceptance criteria)
3. Success metrics (leading and lagging)
4. User flow (step-by-step)
5. Edge cases to handle
6. Out of scope (v2 ideas)
7. Open questions

Tone: Clear and unambiguous, focused on user outcome not features, acknowledging tradeoffs."

**2. User Research Interview Script**
"Act as a user researcher skilled at uncovering deep insights.

Task: Create an interview script for understanding [user behavior/pain point/feature request].

Context: Target users: [segment]. Current hypothesis: [what we think is true]. Goal: [validate/explore/discover]. Time available: [minutes].

Format:
1. Warm-up questions (2-3)
2. Context questions (3-5)
3. Deep dive questions (5-7 with follow-ups)
4. Closing (next steps, thank you)
Include: What to listen for, red flags, probing questions

Tone: Curious and open, avoiding leading questions, building rapport before digging deep."

**3. Feature Prioritization**
"Act as a product strategist who helps teams prioritize ruthlessly.

Task: Help me prioritize these [number] feature requests: [list features].

Context: Business goal: [OKR]. Resources: [team size/quarter]. User feedback: [top complaints]. Revenue impact: [which features unlock deals]. Technical debt: [what's slowing us down].

Let's approach this systematically:
1. First, score each feature on Impact (1-10)
2. Then, estimate Effort (1-10)
3. Then, calculate Impact/Effort ratio
4. Consider dependencies and quick wins
5. Recommend prioritized roadmap with reasoning

Format: Table + narrative explaining the tradeoffs.

Tone: Data-driven but acknowledging uncertainty, focused on outcomes over outputs."

**4. Bug Report Analysis**
"Act as a technical support analyst who triages and prioritizes bugs.

Task: Analyze these [number] bug reports and recommend priority: [paste bug descriptions].

Context: Current sprint capacity: [points]. User impact: [users affected by each]. Revenue at risk: [if any]. Workarounds available: [yes/no for each].

Format:
1. Severity classification (P0/P1/P2/P3) with reasoning
2. Recommended priority order
3. Which bugs can be batched together
4. Which need immediate hotfix
5. Communication plan for affected users

Tone: Balanced between urgency and pragmatism, user-focused."

**5. Technical Documentation**
"Act as a technical writer who makes complex systems understandable.

Task: Write documentation for [API/feature/system].

Context: Audience: [developers/end users/internal team]. Technical level: [beginner/intermediate/advanced]. Most common use cases: [use case 1, 2, 3]. Common mistakes: [pitfall 1, 2].

Format:
1. Overview (what it does, why use it)
2. Quick start (simplest implementation)
3. Common use cases (with code examples)
4. Configuration options (with defaults)
5. Troubleshooting (common errors + fixes)
6. FAQ

Tone: Clear and helpful, assuming no prior knowledge, practical examples over theory."

### [3:30-4:15] CATEGORY 4: FINANCE & OPERATIONS (8 prompts)

**1. Financial Scenario Analysis**
"Act as a financial analyst who creates clear scenario models.

Task: Create a scenario analysis for [decision: hire/investment/expansion].

Context: Current financials: [revenue/costs/runway]. Proposed investment: [amount]. Expected outcomes: Best case [+X%], Base case [+Y%], Worst case [-Z%]. Time horizon: [months/years].

Let's approach this systematically:
1. First, model the base case (current trajectory)
2. Then, model each scenario with assumptions
3. Calculate key metrics (CAC, LTV, payback period, ROI)
4. Identify break-even points
5. Recommend decision with risk assessment

Format: Table + visual summary + decision recommendation.

Tone: Objective and data-driven, acknowledging unknowns, focused on decision-making."

**2. Budget Variance Analysis**
"Act as a CFO reviewing monthly financial performance.

Task: Analyze this budget vs actual variance: [paste numbers or describe].

Context: Department: [dept]. Budget: [amount]. Actual: [amount]. Variance: [±X%]. Time period: [month/quarter]. Business context: [any relevant events].

Format:
1. Variance summary (over/under, by category)
2. Root cause analysis (why each variance occurred)
3. Forecast impact (does this affect yearly targets?)
4. Recommended actions (adjust budget/adjust spending/investigate)
5. Key questions for the team

Tone: Analytical not accusatory, focused on learning and improvement, forward-looking."

**3. Process Optimization**
"Act as an operations consultant who eliminates waste and improves efficiency.

Task: Analyze this business process and recommend improvements: [describe current process].

Context: Process takes [time] and costs [amount]. Pain points: [bottlenecks]. Volume: [frequency]. Team: [who's involved]. Tools: [current systems].

Let's think through this step by step:
1. Map current state (identify all steps)
2. Identify waste (waiting, handoffs, rework, manual)
3. Benchmark (how do others do this?)
4. Design future state (optimized process)
5. Estimate impact (time/cost savings)
6. Implementation plan (quick wins first)

Format: Before/after process map + impact analysis + action plan.

Tone: Pragmatic and implementation-focused, respecting constraints, quick wins before major changes."

**4. Vendor Evaluation**
"Act as a procurement specialist who runs structured vendor selection.

Task: Create an evaluation framework for [software/service category].

Context: Current situation: [problem/need]. Budget: [range]. Must-have requirements: [req 1, 2, 3]. Nice-to-have: [req 4, 5]. Team size: [users]. Integration needs: [systems]. Vendors being considered: [vendor 1, 2, 3].

Format:
1. Evaluation criteria (with weights)
2. Scoring rubric (1-5 for each criteria)
3. Vendor comparison table
4. Total cost of ownership analysis (not just license cost)
5. Risk assessment (vendor stability, lock-in)
6. Recommendation with reasoning

Tone: Thorough and objective, balancing cost with value, considering long-term implications."

**5. KPI Dashboard Design**
"Act as a business intelligence analyst who creates actionable dashboards.

Task: Design a KPI dashboard for [department/role].

Context: Role: [title]. Key responsibilities: [resp 1, 2, 3]. Current reporting: [what they look at now]. Decisions they make: [decision types]. Update frequency needed: [real-time/daily/weekly].

Format:
1. North Star Metric (the one number that matters most)
2. 4-6 Supporting Metrics (leading indicators)
3. For each metric: Definition, Target, Why it matters, What action to take if off-track
4. Data sources and refresh cadence
5. Dashboard layout (hierarchy and grouping)

Tone: User-focused, actionable not just informational, simple before complex."

### [4:15-5:00] CATEGORY 5: HR & PEOPLE OPS (8 prompts)

**1. Job Description That Attracts Talent**
"Act as a recruitment marketing specialist who writes job posts that convert.

Task: Write a job description for [role] at [company].

Context: Company stage: [startup/scaleup/enterprise]. Culture: [key values]. What makes this role unique: [differentiation]. Required skills: [must-haves]. Nice-to-have: [preferences]. Compensation: [range]. Remote: [yes/no/hybrid].

Format:
1. Hook (why this role matters now)
2. About us (2-3 sentences, why work here)
3. What you'll do (4-5 bullets, outcomes not tasks)
4. What we're looking for (must-haves, deal-breakers)
5. Bonus points (nice-to-haves)
6. What we offer (comp, benefits, growth)
7. How to apply (clear next steps)

Tone: Exciting but authentic, inclusive language, focused on impact and growth, transparent about expectations."

**2. Performance Review Prep**
"Act as an HR leader who conducts developmental performance reviews.

Task: Prepare a performance review for [employee name], [role].

Context: Review period: [timeframe]. Key accomplishments: [achievement 1, 2, 3]. Areas for growth: [gap 1, 2]. Feedback received: [from peers/manager/customers]. Goals set last review: [progress on goals]. Career aspirations: [what they want].

Format:
1. Strengths (specific examples with impact)
2. Growth areas (behavior-based, actionable)
3. Goals for next period (SMART goals)
4. Development plan (how we'll support growth)
5. Career path discussion points
6. Compensation discussion (if applicable)

Tone: Balanced and developmental, specific not vague, focused on growth not just evaluation, partnership mindset."

**3. Difficult Conversation Script**
"Act as an executive coach who prepares leaders for challenging conversations.

Task: Help me prepare for a difficult conversation with [employee/team] about [issue].

Context: The situation: [what happened]. Impact: [consequences]. Previous attempts to address: [what's been tried]. Person's typical response: [defensive/receptive/avoidant]. Relationship: [how long working together, trust level]. Desired outcome: [behavior change/clarity/decision].

Format:
1. Opening (how to start the conversation)
2. Situation description (facts, not judgments)
3. Impact explanation (why this matters)
4. Listen and understand (questions to ask)
5. Collaborative solution (involve them in fix)
6. Clear next steps and accountability
7. How to handle defensive reactions

Tone: Direct but respectful, clear about expectations, focused on future not blame, maintaining dignity."

**4. Onboarding Plan (First 90 Days)**
"Act as a People Ops specialist who designs exceptional onboarding experiences.

Task: Create a 90-day onboarding plan for [role].

Context: Role: [title]. Team: [size/structure]. Key relationships: [who they'll work with]. Success looks like: [outcomes by 30/60/90 days]. Common new hire challenges: [typical struggles]. Resources available: [buddy/mentor/training].

Format:
Week-by-week plan with:
1. What they should learn
2. Who they should meet
3. What they should create/ship
4. Check-in points (manager 1-on-1s)
5. Success milestones

For Days 1, 7, 30, 60, 90: Specific success criteria.

Tone: Welcoming and structured, setting up for success, balancing learning with doing."

**5. Team Engagement Survey Analysis**
"Act as an organizational psychologist who interprets engagement data.

Task: Analyze these engagement survey results and recommend actions: [paste scores or describe].

Context: Team: [team name]. Size: [number]. Recent changes: [reorg/new leader/growth]. Survey areas: [engagement dimensions]. Scores: [high/medium/low areas]. Comparison: [vs company average, vs last survey].

Let's approach this systematically:
1. Identify patterns (what themes emerge?)
2. Prioritize issues (impact vs effort to fix)
3. Validate with qualitative feedback (what comments say)
4. Root cause analysis (why are scores what they are?)
5. Action plan (specific, measurable interventions)
6. Communication plan (how to share results and actions)

Tone: Solutions-oriented, acknowledging concerns, focused on team input and co-creation."

### [5:00-5:45] HOW TO BUILD YOUR PERSONAL LIBRARY

**Now you have 50+ prompts. Here's how to make them yours:**

**Step 1: Start with the 5 most relevant to your role**
- Don't try to use all 50 at once
- Pick the tasks you do most frequently
- Test them this week

**Step 2: Customize each prompt**
- Replace generic [brackets] with your specifics
- Adjust tone to match your company culture
- Add context that's unique to your situation
- Save the customized version

**Step 3: Track what works**
- Note which prompts save you the most time
- Refine prompts based on outputs (use iteration!)
- Delete prompts you never use
- Add new prompts as you discover needs

**Step 4: Organize for quick access**
- Save in notes app with search tags
- Create a Notion database
- Use a prompt manager tool (PromptBox, Snipd)
- Keep on printed cheat sheet at desk

**Step 5: Share with your team**
- Best prompts multiply value when team uses them
- Create company-specific prompt library
- Run workshop to teach team the framework
- Collect team's best prompts
